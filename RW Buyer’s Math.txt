RW Buyer’s Math
How to compare two ranked weapons without getting baited by color, hype, or price
If you want the general RW guide, start with post #1. This post is not about re-explaining every bonus from scratch. It is about something much more practical: how to compare two actual ranked weapon listings and decide which one is the better buy.
In other words, this is not a bonus guide. This is a buyer framework. Post #1 answers the question “what is good?” This one answers the harder question: “what is actually worth buying, at this price, for this account, right now?”
A weapon can be strong and still be a bad buy. A weapon can be yellow and still be the smarter purchase. A weapon can be red and still be mostly a luxury item wearing combat clothing. The whole point of RW buyer’s math is to separate combat value from collector hype.
Combat Math: Powerful vs Empower. Often players ask if these are just two ways to do the same thing. Mathematically, they are very different:

1. Empower (Strength): Boosts your raw stats. However, Torn uses a logarithmic formula for stats (e.g., 7 * (LOG(STR/10))^2...). This means Strength has massive diminishing returns. If you already outweigh their defense, adding millions of points via Empower barely changes the final damage.
2. Powerful (Weapon Damage): Boosts the weapon's damage multiplier. This is applied outside the logarithm. It scales perfectly and hits like a truck regardless of how high your stats are.

Verdict: Powerful is king for primary damage, while Empower is a nice stat-padder for secondary/melee slots.
 
1. The 6-part buyer model
Every RW can be judged across six simple categories. Each category gets 0 to 5 points. The total score is out of 30. This is not a perfect formula and it is not supposed to be. It is a decision tool. The goal is not to replace experience. The goal is to stop newer and mid-game players from making obviously bad purchases just because a listing has a shiny red outline and a scary price tag.

2. Start with the base, not the color
This is the part many newer buyers underestimate. Before you even look at the bonus, ask yourself whether the base weapon is already good. A strong base gives you a high floor. A weak base forces the bonus to do all the heavy lifting, and that is rarely where the best value lives.
Damage tells you how hard the weapon hits, accuracy tells you how often that hit is likely to connect cleanly, and quality tells you how good the rolled copy is inside its allowed range.

3. What quality actually tells you
Quality is not the same thing as bonus usefulness, and it is not the same thing as rarity. In practical terms, quality tells you how good the rolled copy is in terms of its damage and accuracy compared with what that base can potentially roll.
The biggest buyer mistake here is simple: people confuse quality with practical strength. A 150% quality Dagger is still a Dagger. A 30% quality ArmaLite is still an ArmaLite. Quality should slightly raise or lower your opinion of the weapon, but it should almost never replace role fit, base strength, or price efficiency.
 
4. Bonus usefulness by frequency
A bonus is only as good as the number of times it actually triggers in a fight. Paying a premium for a bonus that requires the stars to align is a fast way to go broke. You want workhorse bonuses that do their job consistently.

Final note
If post #1 was about what is good, this post is about what is worth buying. Those are not always the same question.
A weapon can be strong and still be a bad buy. A weapon can be yellow and still be the smarter purchase. The whole point of RW buyer’s math is to separate combat value from collector hype.
If you score two listings side by side with this model, you will not make every decision perfectly. But you will usually make a much smarter one.
